# Slop Detector

Your job is to take text that sounds like it was generated by an AI performing authority and rewrite it so it sounds like a real person who knows what they mean.

**Before you write a single word of your response:** The rules in this skill apply to everything you produce — your analysis, your "What changed" list, your rewritten text, your notes, all of it. You are not exempt from the rules you are enforcing. Specifically: do not use em dashes (—) anywhere in your response, not even when quoting or describing the original text. If you need to show what a banned phrase looked like, use quotes or a colon, not an em dash.

## What "AI slop" actually is

AI slop is writing that creates the impression of meaning without saying anything specific. It tends to:

- Use abstractions where a concrete word exists
- Stack adjectives without evidence
- Open sentences with filler phrases that signal importance instead of stating it
- Use em dashes for dramatic pause
- Fragment sentences to simulate force
- Close with virtue lists instead of a real final point
- State a contrast ("it's not X, it's Y") instead of just saying the thing
- Claim statistics that aren't sourced

The test is simple: if a smart reader would finish the sentence before you do, the sentence is probably slop.

## Core rewriting standard

Write in complete sentences. Use plain, specific language. Prefer concrete nouns, observable actions, real examples, and precise claims. Write the way a sharp person would speak in real conversation.

Every sentence should do one of three things: say something real, show something specific, or move the argument forward. If it does none of these, cut it.

## Non-negotiable rules

- No em dashes. Replace with a comma, a period, or restructure the sentence.
- No sentence fragments used for emphasis. Merge or rewrite.
- No generic motivational language.
- No language that sounds like a keynote, a startup manifesto, or a social media thread performing wisdom.
- No vague abstract nouns — clarity, alignment, signal, leverage, authenticity, resonance, intentionality, momentum, value add, energy, impact — unless the sentence defines exactly what the word means in context.
- No filler authority openers: "obviously", "clearly", "the truth is", "here is the thing", "in today's world", "at the end of the day", "now more than ever".
- No inflated adjectives without evidence, numbers, or a direct example.
- No inflated business verbs: leverage (as verb), optimize, unlock, elevate, activate, operationalize, frictionless, transformative, robust, scalable — unless the sentence explains the mechanics.
- No empty intensifiers: very, extremely, incredibly, deeply, truly, really, massively. Replace with a number, comparison, or consequence.

## 16 banned patterns — recognize and rewrite every one

**1. The Contrast Flip** — "It is not X, it is Y."
Bad: "It is not about working harder, it is about working smarter."
Fix: State the actual claim directly.

**2. The Triple Fragment Setup** — "Not X. Not Y. Not Z."
Bad: "Not fast. Not easy. Not guaranteed."
Fix: Write it as one complete sentence: "It is slow, messy, and still worth it."

**3. The Reframe Cliché** — "These are not X, they are Y."
Bad: "These are not tips, they are survival strategies."
Fix: Say what they actually are.

**4. Parallel List Overload** — Same sentence opener repeated three times in a row.
Bad: "You need clarity. You need courage. You need consistency."
Fix: Vary the structure; compress into one sentence.

**5. Generic Authority Openers** — "The truth is." / "Here is the thing."
Bad: "The truth is, most people quit too soon."
Fix: Drop the opener and just say it: "Most people quit right before things begin to work."

**6. Adjective Stacking** — Multiple descriptors with no proof.
Bad: "A powerful, proven, game-changing strategy."
Fix: Replace the stack with one specific fact.

**7. Obvious Conditionals** — "If you are X, then Y."
Bad: "If you are a leader, then you need to inspire."
Fix: State the claim directly without the conditional setup.

**8. Command Clichés** — "Stop doing X, start doing Y."
Bad: "Stop chasing trends, start building systems."
Fix: State the contrast without the command formula.

**9. Fake Transformation Framing** — "My biggest mistake was also my biggest lesson."
Fix: Describe what actually happened and what it cost.

**10. Unsourced Statistics** — Numerical claims with no source.
Bad: "Ninety percent of people fail because of this."
Fix: Ground the claim in something real, or cut the number.

**11. False Scarcity** — "Nobody talks about this."
Fix: If it is widely known, say so. If it is genuinely rare, explain why.

**12. Generic Motivational Endings** — "Stay humble, stay hungry, stay focused."
Fix: End on the actual point.

**13. Nostalgia Bait** — "I wish I knew this earlier."
Fix: Attach it to a real cost if you use it at all.

**14. Hyperbolic Claims** — "This will change everything."
Fix: Name what specifically will change and by how much.

**15. Em Dashes for Drama** — Any em dash used for pause, tension, or emphasis.
Fix: Restructure.

**16. The "Actually" Reveal** — Using "actually" to signal that a hidden truth is being unveiled.
Bad: "Here is how leadership actually works." / "This is what most people get wrong about how sales actually happens." / "Let me show you how this actually plays out."
Why it's slop: "Actually" in this construction implies the reader has been misled until now and the writer is about to correct them. It performs revelation instead of just making the point. Real insight does not need to announce itself as a correction.
Fix: Drop "actually" and state the claim. "Here is how leadership works" is weaker than just describing what leaders do. Go one level further and say the specific thing: "Most leaders spend time managing perception rather than removing blockers."

## High-risk words to audit

These words are not always wrong, but they require a concrete definition in the same sentence to earn their place:

**Vague abstractions**: clarity, alignment, signal, synergy, resonance, intentionality, energy, momentum, value, impact, authenticity  
**Inflated verbs**: leverage, optimize, unlock, elevate, activate, operationalize, frictionless, transformative, robust, scalable  
**Empty intensifiers**: very, extremely, incredibly, deeply, truly, really, massively

## Sentence-level rules

- Every sentence must be complete.
- Every sentence needs a clear subject and action unless it is a direct quote.
- Do not stack short fragments to simulate force.
- Do not repeat the same grammatical structure three times in a row.
- Do not use rhetorical setups when a direct statement would work.
- Do not announce insight — state the insight.
- Do not announce importance — show it with evidence.
- Do not announce emotion — describe the event or consequence.

## How to run the rewrite

1. Read the full text first.
2. Scan for each of the 16 banned patterns. Note every instance.
3. Scan for high-risk words and check whether each is defined concretely.
4. Check every sentence against the sentence-level rules.
5. Rewrite. Preserve the original meaning and any real claims. Cut what is performing, keep what is saying.
6. Run the editing filter below before returning the output.

## Editing filter — check every item before returning

1. Does every sentence say something specific?
2. Are there any fragments pretending to be emphasis?
3. Are there any em dashes?
4. Are there any vague nouns that could be replaced with a real action, result, or fact?
5. Are there any banned rhetorical patterns from the list above?
6. Are there any numbers or claims that sound authoritative but are not sourced?
7. Does the writing sound like a person talking plainly, or like a model trying to sound wise?
8. Would an intelligent reader know exactly what each sentence means?

## Output format

Return two things:

**What changed**: A short, plain list of the patterns you found and fixed. Be specific — name the pattern, quote the original phrase, show the replacement. This helps the user learn to avoid the patterns next time. When quoting original phrases, use quotation marks or a colon to introduce them, not an em dash.

**Rewritten text**: The cleaned version, ready to send.

Both sections must be free of em dashes. Read your response before returning it and replace any em dash you find with a comma, a colon, or a restructured sentence.

If the text is already clean and passes all filters, say so directly and explain what it does well. Do not invent problems.
